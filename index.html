The National Parks Project

“There is nothing so American as our national parks…. The fundamental idea behind the parks…is that the country belongs to the people, that it is in process of making for the enrichment of the lives of all of us.” - President Franklin D. Roosevelt

In 1872, Congress created the first national park: Yellowstone National Park. 44 years later, on on August 25, 1916, President Woodrow Wilson established the National Park Service. These two events launched one of the most ambitious and beneficial projects in our nation's history. The National Park System represents some of the best parts of our country, preserving our natural beauty for everyone to enjoy. 

However, this system is not perfect. The national parks are often held on stolen land, taken from indigenous peoples who were never fairly compensated, in a situation where no compensation could ever validate the theft of a home. National parks are also disproportionately enjoyed by white Americans, and undervisited among communities of color. Lost in the beauty of these parks and the overall positivity of this system are these issues and many others.

This website aims to tell two stories: the history of the National Parks system and the story of the opposite perspective, for which the benefit of the parks is not felt in nearly the same way. As the author, I have chosen to focus on a select few parks on the west coast that I have visited (Zion, Yosemite, and Joshua Tree), and each page will attempt to tell both stories, along with images that I have captured there. I selected these three parks because they each have a different type of history, which translates into different modern-day approaches to honoring their pasts. When reading more about each park, I hope that the information presented serves as a launching point for readers to learn more about the histories of some of their favorite places. While we cannot change the past, we can do our best to honor it in the present day. To find the information that inspired this project, as well as resources for further research, visit the Resources page.
